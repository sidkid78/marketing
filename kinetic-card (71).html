The Legacy of Life Restored

The Virologist’s
Sanctuary

In the early 1950s, the world was a place of quiet terror. Parents kept their children indoors, fearing an invisible predator: Polio. Inside a sterile lab at the University of Pittsburgh, Dr. L. James Lewis worked alongside Jonas Salk to hunt that predator down. He was the man responsible for identifying the virus strains—the microscopic “beasts” that had paralyzed a generation.

A Pair of Leopards

This artwork, A Pair of Leopards, was Dr. Lewis’s personal window into a world he helped save.

Acquired during his research travels in South Africa, these leopards represent a profound irony. While Dr. Lewis spent his hours peering through a microscope at the world's most dangerous invisible threats, he chose to live his private life in the company of nature’s most magnificent visible ones.

The Art of Precision

The lithograph is encased in a custom Verre Églomisé frame—a technique as meticulous as the science Dr. Lewis practiced. Just as he layered data to create a vaccine, a master craftsman hand-applied gold leaf to the reverse of the glass to create this shimmering, geometric border.

It is a protective barrier of gold and glass, shielding a moment of wild beauty.

The Provenance of Hope

This is not merely a mid-century print. It is an artifact from the private quarters of a man who changed the course of human history. To own this piece is to hold a fragment of the quiet, refined taste of a hero who traded the cold fluorescent lights of the laboratory for the warm, golden glow of the South African veldt.

A Note for the Steward

“Because this work features the delicate Verre Églomisé gilding, it must be treated with the same reverence Dr. Lewis gave his laboratory cultures. No chemicals. No moisture. Only a dry, gentle touch to preserve the gold that frames this vision of a world made safe.”
